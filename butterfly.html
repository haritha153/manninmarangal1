Butterfly Pea Plant

Clitoria ternatea

The Magical Blue Flower of Southeast Asia

Basic Information

The Butterfly Pea plant (Clitoria ternatea), also known as Blue Pea or Asian Pigeonwings, is a perennial herbaceous vine native to tropical equatorial Asia. Famous for its vibrant blue flowers, this plant has garnered worldwide popularity for both its ornamental beauty and remarkable medicinal and culinary applications. It has been an integral part of traditional Ayurvedic medicine and is now celebrated for its natural blue dye that changes color with pH variations.

Scientific Classification

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Family: Fabaceae
  • Genus: Clitoria
  • Species: C. ternatea

Names in Different Languages

  • Thai: อัญชัน (Anchan)
  • Malay: Bunga Telang
  • Hindi: अपराजिता (Aparajita)
  • Sanskrit: शंखपुष्पी (Shankhpushpi)
  • Filipino: Pukinggan
  • Burmese: ကချင်ပြည်နယ်လိပ်ပြာ (Kachin)

Physical Characteristics

  • Height: Climbing vine reaching 2-3 meters (6-10 feet)
  • Leaves: Elliptical, pinnate with 5-7 leaflets
  • Flowers: Deep blue to purple, occasionally white
  • Fruit: Flat, beaked pods containing 6-10 seeds
  • Lifespan: Perennial in tropical regions

Cultural & Traditional Significance

The Butterfly Pea has rich cultural heritage across various Asian countries, where it has been used in traditional practices for centuries:

Thailand

In Thailand, butterfly pea flower (Dok Anchan) is used to color traditional desserts and drinks. The blue rice dessert "Kao Niew Look Anchan" is particularly popular during festivals and celebrations.

Malaysia

Known as Bunga Telang, the flower is used to color rice dishes like Nasi Kerabu, a blue-colored rice popular in the eastern states of Malaysia. It's also used in traditional medicine.

Indian Traditions

In India, the plant is known as Aparajita and is associated with Goddess Durga. The flowers are often offered in religious ceremonies, and the plant is revered in Ayurvedic medicine for its cognitive benefits.

Ayurvedic Medicine

In traditional Ayurveda, butterfly pea has been used for centuries as "Medhya Rasayana" - a brain tonic that enhances memory, intelligence, and combats stress and anxiety.

Medicinal Uses

The Butterfly Pea plant has been used in traditional medicine systems across Asia for various health applications:

Butterfly Pea Flowers

  • Rich in anthocyanins with potent antioxidant properties
  • Used to enhance memory and cognitive function
  • Helps reduce stress and anxiety
  • Anti-inflammatory and fever-reducing properties
  • Used to treat eye infections and improve vision

Butterfly Pea Roots

  • Have diuretic properties
  • Used to treat urinary disorders
  • Applied to treat skin diseases
  • Help relieve inflammation in joints

Butterfly Pea Seeds

  • Used as a purgative
  • Applied to treat enlarged spleen
  • Help in controlling blood sugar levels
  • Used as an aphrodisiac in some traditional systems

Butterfly Pea Leaves

  • Used to treat skin diseases and swelling
  • Applied externally for inflammation
  • Help in relieving ear pain
  • Used in hair care formulations to prevent graying

Modern Applications

Beyond its traditional uses, the Butterfly Pea has found numerous applications in contemporary contexts:

Natural Food Coloring

The brilliant blue extract from butterfly pea flowers is increasingly being used as a natural alternative to synthetic blue food colorings, particularly appealing in the clean-label food movement.

Color-Changing Beverages

The pH-sensitive anthocyanins in butterfly pea flowers create magical color-changing effects in drinks, turning from blue to purple or pink when acidic ingredients like lemon are added.

Cosmetic Industry

Extracts are used in natural skincare and haircare products for their antioxidant properties and ability to enhance appearance of hair and skin.

Scientific Research

There's growing scientific interest in butterfly pea's bioactive compounds for potential applications in managing diabetes, cognitive disorders, and inflammatory conditions.

Growing & Cultivation

The Butterfly Pea plant is relatively easy to grow and can be a beautiful addition to gardens in suitable climates:

Climate Requirements

Thrives in tropical and subtropical climates. Grows best in USDA Hardiness Zones 9-11, where winter temperatures rarely drop below freezing.

Soil & Sunlight

Prefers well-draining, fertile soil with a pH of 6.5-7.5. Requires full sun to partial shade, with at least 6 hours of direct sunlight daily for optimal flowering.

Watering & Care

Moderate watering is required, allowing soil to dry slightly between waterings. Established plants are somewhat drought-tolerant but flower best with consistent moisture.

Propagation

Easily propagated from seeds or stem cuttings. Seeds should be soaked overnight before planting to enhance germination. Vines benefit from trellises or supports to climb.

Culinary Uses

The Butterfly Pea flower has gained worldwide popularity in culinary applications:

Butterfly Pea Tea

The dried flowers are steeped in hot water to create a naturally blue herbal tea with subtle earthy flavor. Adding lemon transforms it into a vibrant purple beverage rich in antioxidants.

Blue Rice

In Southeast Asian cuisine, particularly in Malaysia and Thailand, butterfly pea extract is used to color rice dishes like Nasi Kerabu, creating visually striking blue rice.

Cocktails & Beverages

The color-changing properties make it a favorite among mixologists for creating "magical" cocktails that transform colors when ingredients are added.

Desserts

Used to naturally color desserts like puddings, ice cream, and Thai sweet jellies (Lod Chong) without artificial additives.

Modern Gastronomy

Innovative chefs incorporate butterfly pea coloring in avant-garde cuisine, creating striking visual presentations in both sweet and savory dishes.

Health Drinks

Often combined with lemon and honey to create health tonics believed to boost immunity, improve vision, and enhance cognitive function.

Interesting Facts

Natural pH Indicator

The butterfly pea flower serves as a natural pH indicator, changing from blue in neutral environments to purple and pink in acidic conditions, making it useful in educational settings.

The Name's Origin

The genus name "Clitoria" was given by botanist Carl Linnaeus due to the flower's resemblance to female genitalia, making it one of the more unusually named plants in botany.

Ancient Tradition

The use of butterfly pea as a memory enhancer and brain tonic in Ayurvedic tradition dates back over 3,000 years, making it one of the oldest documented natural nootropics.

Natural Nitrogen Fixer

As a legume, butterfly pea enriches soil by fixing atmospheric nitrogen through symbiotic bacteria in its root nodules, making it beneficial for companion planting.

Growing Popularity

Since 2016, international interest in butterfly pea has surged by over 500%, driven by social media trends featuring color-changing beverages and blue foods.

Butterfly Connection

Despite its name, the butterfly pea doesn't particularly attract butterflies. Its name comes from the shape of the flower which resembles a butterfly in flight.
