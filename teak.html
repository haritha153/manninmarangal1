Teak Tree (Tectona grandis)

The teak tree is a large deciduous tree native to South and Southeast Asia, especially India, Myanmar, Thailand, and Laos. It is highly valued for its durable and water-resistant wood, making it one of the most important timber trees in the world.

Interesting Facts about Teak

  • Exceptional Durability: Teak wood contains natural oils and silica, making it highly resistant to water, pests, and rot. This is why it is widely used in shipbuilding and outdoor furniture.
  • Long Lifespan: Teak trees can live for over 100 years, and some plantations harvest trees only after 60-80 years for the best quality wood.
  • Golden Brown Wood: Freshly cut teak wood is yellowish, but it matures to a rich golden brown over time due to oxidation and exposure to sunlight.
  • Medicinal Uses: In traditional medicine, teak leaves and bark have been used to treat headaches, wounds, and skin diseases.
  • Ecological Role: Teak plantations can help restore degraded lands and provide habitat for various species, though natural teak forests are increasingly rare.
  • National Symbol: Teak is the national tree of Myanmar (Burma), where some of the world's largest and oldest teak trees are found.

Did you know? The world's largest teak tree, known as "Minhla Taung," is located in Myanmar and is estimated to be over 1,500 years old!
