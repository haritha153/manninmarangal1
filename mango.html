Mango Tree

Mangifera indica

The King of Fruits and National Tree of India

Basic Information

The Mango tree (Mangifera indica) is an evergreen tree indigenous to the Indian subcontinent and Southeast Asia. Known as the "King of Fruits," the mango tree has been cultivated for over 4,000 years and holds significant cultural, religious, and economic importance across tropical and subtropical regions worldwide.

Scientific Classification

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Family: Anacardiaceae
  • Genus: Mangifera
  • Species: M. indica

Names in Different Languages

  • Tamil: மாமரம் (Maamaram)
  • Hindi: आम (Aam)
  • Sanskrit: आम्र (Amra)
  • Malayalam: മാവ് (Maavu)
  • Telugu: మామిడి (Mamidi)
  • Kannada: ಮಾವಿನ ಮರ (Mavina Mara)

Physical Characteristics

  • Height: Up to 40 meters (130 feet)
  • Leaves: Evergreen, lance-shaped, dark green
  • Fruit: Fleshy drupe in various sizes, shapes, and colors
  • Bark: Dark brown, rough with numerous cracks
  • Lifespan: Can live over 300 years

Cultural & Historical Significance

The Mango tree holds profound cultural and historical importance across various traditions:

Hinduism

Mango leaves are considered auspicious and are used in religious ceremonies, festivals, and weddings. The fruit is associated with Kama, the god of love. Mango blossoms are believed to be the arrows used by Kama.

Buddhism

Buddha was said to have been given a mango grove named Amravana where he could rest. Several Buddhist legends mention mango trees and their fruits.

National Symbol

The Mango is the national fruit of India, Pakistan, and the Philippines. In India, it is also the national tree of Bangladesh and is closely associated with Indian culture.

Tamil Culture

In Tamil Nadu, mango leaves are used to decorate entrances during festivals and celebrations. The tree's wood is considered sacred and used in religious ceremonies and traditional furniture making.

Medicinal Uses

Nearly every part of the mango tree possesses medicinal properties and has been used in traditional medicine systems like Ayurveda, Siddha, and Unani:

Mango Leaves

  • Treatment for diabetes and blood pressure regulation
  • Antibacterial properties for treating respiratory issues
  • Help manage anxiety and restlessness
  • Used to treat burns and scalds when applied externally

Mango Bark

  • Treats diarrhea and dysentery
  • Used for urinary tract infections
  • Has astringent properties
  • Helps in treating hemorrhages and bleeding disorders

Mango Fruit

  • Rich in vitamins A, C, and E
  • Boosts immunity and improves digestion
  • Contains enzymes that aid in breaking down proteins
  • High antioxidant content beneficial for skin health

Mango Seeds

  • Used to treat kidney stones
  • Possess anti-inflammatory properties
  • Help in treating asthma and cough
  • Useful in managing diarrhea and digestive issues

Economic Importance

The Mango tree is of significant economic value worldwide:

Global Production

India is the largest producer of mangoes, accounting for approximately 50% of the world's production, followed by China, Thailand, Indonesia, and Mexico.

Culinary Uses

Beyond fresh consumption, mangoes are used in various food products including juices, pickles, chutneys, jams, candies, dried fruits, and as flavoring agents.

Timber Value

Mango wood is valuable for furniture, flooring, musical instruments, and charcoal. It's known for its durability and distinctive grain pattern.

Employment

The mango industry provides employment to millions of people worldwide in cultivation, harvesting, processing, transportation, and marketing.

Popular Varieties in India

India alone cultivates over 1,500 varieties of mangoes, each with unique characteristics:

Alphonso

Known as "King of Mangoes," primarily grown in Maharashtra. Famous for its rich, sweet flavor, smooth texture, and minimal fiber content.

Banganapalli

Popular in South India, especially Andhra Pradesh. Has a fibreless pulp, sweet taste, and golden yellow color when ripe.

Langra

Primarily grown in northern India. Known for its distinct aroma, sweet taste, and fibrous pulp. Remains green even when ripe.

Neelam

Popular in Tamil Nadu and Andhra Pradesh. Medium-sized with a distinctive aroma and yellow-orange flesh when ripe.

Dasheri

Originated in Uttar Pradesh. Characterized by its unique aroma, sweet taste, and low fiber content. Has a distinctive curved shape.

Malgova

Famous in Tamil Nadu. Large-sized with a sweet-sour taste and distinct aroma. Often consumed when slightly unripe due to its unique flavor.

Interesting Facts

Ancient Origins

Mangoes have been cultivated for over 4,000 years in India, with evidence of mango cultivation dating back to 2000 BCE in the Indian subcontinent.

Family Relations

Mangoes belong to the same family (Anacardiaceae) as cashews, pistachios, and poison ivy. The sap of the mango tree can cause skin irritation in sensitive individuals.

Record Numbers

The oldest mango tree is believed to be over 300 years old and is located in Andhra Pradesh, India. It still bears fruit annually.

Mango Festivals

India hosts numerous mango festivals annually, with the International Mango Festival in Delhi being one of the most famous, showcasing hundreds of varieties.

Global Spread

Portuguese explorers introduced mangoes to Africa in the 16th century, while the first mango tree in the Americas was planted in Brazil around 1700.

Mango Madness

In traditional Indian households, the arrival of mango season is celebrated as a festive occasion, with special recipes and rituals dedicated to the first mangoes of the season.
